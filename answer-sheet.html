Drug Education Jeopardy!

Complete Facilitator Answer Guide & Discussion Resource
Designed for Boys Ages 13–19 | Group Home Setting

Facilitator Guide: Before You Begin

This game is designed to build real, practical knowledge about drugs, addiction, and staying safe — not to shame or lecture. The young men in this room may have personal or family experience with substance use. Approach every topic with respect and curiosity.

  • Two rounds of play. Round 1 covers foundational topics (alcohol, drug types, health effects, myths, prevention, recognizing signs). Round 2 goes deeper (fentanyl crisis, addiction science, treatment, legal consequences, family impact, real-life decision making).
  • After each answer is revealed, a Discussion Point appears on screen. Use it to spark a brief conversation before moving to the next question. These moments are where the real learning happens.
  • Not a quiz — a conversation starter. If a team gets a question wrong, don't move on too fast. Ask the group what they thought the answer was and why. Wrong answers reveal what people actually believe.
  • Personal disclosures. Some questions may prompt personal sharing about family members, past use, or trauma. Be ready to hold space for that — and know when to refer to a counselor.
  • Daily Doubles and Final Jeopardy add drama and engagement. Let teams celebrate and compete — the fun is part of what makes information stick.
Trauma-Informed Tip: Some participants may become quiet or uncomfortable with questions about family addiction, overdose, or legal consequences. Give space. You might say: "You don't have to answer this one personally — let's just talk about what the facts tell us." Never force participation.
Before playing, post these in the room: National Helpline: 1-800-662-4357 (SAMHSA)  |  Crisis Line: 988  |  Crisis Text: Text HOME to 741741

⚠️ Crisis Resources — Keep These Visible During the Game

  • SAMHSA National Helpline: 1-800-662-4357 — Free, confidential, 24/7. Treatment referrals.
  • 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline: Call or text 988 — Mental health AND substance crisis support.
  • Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741 — For anyone in crisis who prefers text.
  • FindTreatment.gov: Search for local treatment by zip code. Many programs are free or Medicaid-covered.
  • Naloxone (Narcan): Available without prescription at most pharmacies. Reverses opioid overdose.

Discussion Starter Questions (Use Between Rounds or After Specific Topics)

On Peer Pressure & Decision Making:

  • "What's the hardest part about saying no when someone you respect is offering you something?"
  • "If you knew for sure a pill had fentanyl in it, would you take it? What if you weren't sure?"
  • "What would it look like to be the person in a group who doesn't use — and still be respected?"

On Addiction & Family:

  • "We learned addiction is a brain disease. How does knowing that change how you feel about someone in your family who uses?"
  • "What does it feel like to grow up in a house where a parent or family member uses drugs or alcohol?"
  • "If you have family history of addiction, does that change anything about how you think about your own choices?"

On Help & Recovery:

  • "What would it take for you to ask for help if you were struggling with something like this?"
  • "Who is one person in your life you could actually call if you were in trouble?"
  • "What does 'getting better' look like to you? What would your life look like if it was going really well?"

On Safety (Narcan / Overdose Response):

  • "If you saw someone overdosing and you were scared of getting in trouble — what would help you make the call to 911 anyway?"
  • "Did you know you could get Narcan free at a pharmacy without a prescription? Would you carry it?"

Alcohol: The Real Deal Round 1 · Category 1
$200
Question:
The legal drinking age in the United States.
Answer:
What is 21?
Discussion Point:
The US set 21 because the brain is still developing through your early 20s. Alcohol hits harder and causes more lasting damage the younger you are.
$400
Question:
This organ in your body has to process almost all the alcohol you drink.
Answer:
What is the liver?
Discussion Point:
Your liver can only handle about one drink per hour. Drinking more doesn't speed up sobering — only time does. Coffee and cold showers don't work.
$600
Question:
Alcohol is classified as this type of drug because it SLOWS your brain and nervous system down.
Answer:
What is a depressant?
Discussion Point:
Even though alcohol can feel like it loosens you up at first, it's slowing your reaction time, judgment, and coordination. That's why drunk people can't drive safely.
$800
Question:
Drinking 4–5 or more drinks in about 2 hours is called this.
Answer:
What is binge drinking?
Discussion Point:
About 1 in 6 adults binge drinks regularly. Binge drinking is responsible for more than half of all alcohol-related deaths.
$1,000
Question:
Alcohol use before age 15 increases the risk of later developing alcohol addiction by this much compared to those who wait until 21.
Answer:
What is 4 times greater (400%)?
Discussion Point:
This is biological, not just about 'bad choices.' The younger a brain is exposed to alcohol, the more it rewires toward addiction. Waiting even a few years makes a huge difference.
Drugs: Know What You're Dealing With Round 1 · Category 2
$200
Question:
Drugs like cocaine and meth that speed up your heart rate and make you feel wired and energetic are called this.
Answer:
What are stimulants?
Discussion Point:
Stimulants raise your heart rate and blood pressure dangerously. Even first-time users have died from heart attacks — including young, healthy people.
$400
Question:
TRUE OR FALSE: A prescription pill from a friend is safer than a street drug because it came from a pharmacy.
Answer:
What is FALSE? Many fake prescription pills are made with fentanyl.
Discussion Point:
The DEA reports that 6 out of 10 fake prescription pills contain a lethal dose of fentanyl. There is NO way to tell by looking at them.
$600
Question:
This drug is 50 to 100 times stronger than morphine and is now found mixed into many street drugs — often without the buyer knowing.
Answer:
What is fentanyl?
Discussion Point:
A lethal dose of fentanyl is 2 milligrams — smaller than a few grains of salt. It's been found in fake pills, cocaine, meth, and even marijuana.
$800
Question:
This dangerous combination called a 'speedball' — mixing a stimulant and an opioid — has killed multiple celebrities.
Answer:
What is cocaine and heroin (or meth and heroin)?
Discussion Point:
Stimulants mask the warning signs of opioid overdose. You don't feel as drowsy, so you take more. When the stimulant wears off first, you overdose from the opioid still in your system.
$1,000
Question:
This percentage of cocaine sold on the street now contains fentanyl — meaning a cocaine user can overdose on opioids they didn't know they were taking.
Answer:
What is about 70%?
Discussion Point:
Many cocaine users don't carry Narcan because they don't think they're using opioids. But with fentanyl contamination this high, Narcan saves cocaine users' lives too.
What Drugs Actually Do to Your Body Round 1 · Category 3
$200
Question:
This life-saving medication can reverse an opioid overdose by blocking opioids from receptors in the brain.
Answer:
What is naloxone (Narcan)?
Discussion Point:
Narcan is available without a prescription in most states. It has no effect if opioids aren't present, so it's safe to give to anyone you suspect is overdosing. It can save a life in minutes.
$400
Question:
Opioids kill by causing this — where breathing slows and stops because the brain 'forgets' to breathe.
Answer:
What is respiratory depression (stopped breathing)?
Discussion Point:
This is why overdosing people turn blue and can't be woken up. Narcan reverses this in minutes. Calling 911 and giving Narcan are the two most important things you can do.
$600
Question:
This condition happens when your body gets so used to a drug that you need more and more to get the same effect.
Answer:
What is tolerance?
Discussion Point:
Tolerance is one of the earliest signs of developing dependence. It's your body physically adapting to the drug — a danger sign, not a badge of honor.
$800
Question:
Meth use causes this vital muscle — your body's pump — to age 10 years faster than normal, increasing heart attack risk enormously.
Answer:
What is the heart?
Discussion Point:
Meth forces your heart to beat faster and harder for extended periods. Even young meth users have had fatal heart attacks. The damage is often permanent.
$1,000
Question:
Repeated opioid overdoses that cut off oxygen to the brain — even ones survived — cause this type of permanent damage.
Answer:
What is brain damage (hypoxic brain injury)?
Discussion Point:
Every non-fatal overdose is a near-death event that can cause permanent cognitive damage. Some survivors have lasting memory, personality, or motor function problems.
Myths vs. Facts: What's Real Round 1 · Category 4
$200
Question:
TRUE OR FALSE: Addiction is a character flaw — people become addicted because they're weak or have no willpower.
Answer:
What is FALSE? Addiction is a chronic brain disease.
Discussion Point:
The American Medical Association, APA, and WHO all classify addiction as a medical disorder. Judging addicts as 'weak' prevents people from getting life-saving treatment.
$400
Question:
TRUE OR FALSE: You can tell if a pill or powder has fentanyl in it by looking at it, tasting it, or smelling it.
Answer:
What is FALSE? You cannot detect fentanyl without a test strip.
Discussion Point:
Fentanyl is odorless, colorless, and tasteless. Even drug dealers sometimes don't know their supply contains it. The only way to test is with fentanyl test strips.
$600
Question:
TRUE OR FALSE: Most teens your age are using drugs or drinking regularly.
Answer:
What is FALSE? Most teens don't use drugs.
Discussion Point:
Only about 6.5% of high school seniors use illicit drugs regularly. The majority of teens choose not to use — even if it doesn't feel that way in some settings.
$800
Question:
TRUE OR FALSE: Using drugs once or twice is safe — you can't get addicted from just trying something.
Answer:
What is FALSE? One use can be fatal with fentanyl.
Discussion Point:
One pill can kill — this isn't exaggeration. Fentanyl has killed people on their very first use. First use is also when many people begin the path to addiction.
$1,000
Question:
TRUE OR FALSE: Teens in the foster care and group home system have the same rates of substance use as the general teen population.
Answer:
What is FALSE? System-involved youth have significantly higher rates.
Discussion Point:
Research shows youth in the foster care system are 2–3 times more likely to develop substance use problems, largely due to trauma, instability, and lack of supportive adults. This is why education matters SO much.
Prevention & Making Good Choices Round 1 · Category 5
$200
Question:
Research shows this is the single most important protective factor against teen drug use.
Answer:
What is having a strong connection to family or a caring adult?
Discussion Point:
Teens who have even ONE consistent, caring adult in their life have dramatically lower rates of drug use. Connection protects. Every adult in this room can be that person for someone.
$400
Question:
These skills — like exercising, talking to someone, journaling, or breathing exercises — help manage stress without substances.
Answer:
What are healthy coping skills?
Discussion Point:
Drugs are often used to cope with stress, boredom, trauma, or pain. Building healthy alternatives for managing hard feelings is one of the most important life skills you can develop.
$600
Question:
Participating in these activities after school or in the evenings reduces drug use risk by about 50%.
Answer:
What are sports, clubs, jobs, or other structured activities?
Discussion Point:
Idle time and boredom are significant risk factors. Activities provide purpose, connection, and positive outlet for stress — the same things drugs promise but can't deliver.
$800
Question:
Teens with undiagnosed or untreated depression, anxiety, ADHD, or trauma are this many times more likely to develop substance use problems.
Answer:
What is twice as likely (2×)?
Discussion Point:
Many people self-medicate real pain with drugs. Treating the underlying mental health issue dramatically reduces substance use. If you're struggling, telling a counselor or doctor matters.
$1,000
Question:
Knowing that first drug use before this age dramatically increases lifetime addiction risk is an important reason why waiting matters.
Answer:
What is age 15 (using before 15 quadruples risk)?
Discussion Point:
This isn't about being 'good.' The brain is physically more vulnerable when it's still developing. Waiting even 2–3 more years significantly reduces your biological risk of addiction.
Recognizing Signs & What To Do Round 1 · Category 6
$200
Question:
These are the signs of an opioid overdose — what you should look for if you think someone is overdosing.
Answer:
What are: slow/stopped breathing, blue lips, unresponsive, pinpoint pupils?
Discussion Point:
You only have minutes. If someone is breathing fewer than once every 5 seconds or not at all — give Narcan immediately and call 911. Stay with them.
$400
Question:
This is the FIRST thing you should do if you think someone near you is overdosing.
Answer:
What is call 911 (and give Narcan if available)?
Discussion Point:
Don't wait to see if they get better. Every minute without oxygen causes brain damage. Most states have Good Samaritan laws — you won't get in trouble for calling for help.
$600
Question:
This position — placing an unconscious person on their side — can save their life by preventing them from choking on vomit.
Answer:
What is the recovery position?
Discussion Point:
Unconscious intoxicated people frequently vomit and can choke on it. Placing them on their side takes 10 seconds and can be the difference between life and death.
$800
Question:
Many states have this law that protects people from prosecution when they call 911 for an overdose — making it safer to get help.
Answer:
What is the Good Samaritan law (or Medical Amnesty law)?
Discussion Point:
Fear of getting in trouble is one reason people don't call 911 during an overdose. Good Samaritan laws exist specifically to remove this barrier.
$1,000
Question:
This national helpline — 988 or 1-800-662-HELP — provides 24/7 free and confidential support for anyone struggling with mental health or substance use.
Answer:
What is the SAMHSA National Helpline or 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline?
Discussion Point:
You don't have to be in a crisis to call. You can call for advice, to talk, or to help a friend. It's free, private, and staffed by real people who care.

The Fentanyl Crisis Round 2 · Category 1
$400
Question:
A lethal dose of fentanyl is about this size — smaller than a few grains of salt or the head on a coin.
Answer:
What is 2 milligrams (about the size of Lincoln's head on a penny)?
Discussion Point:
You cannot see 2 milligrams. You cannot smell or taste it. This is why 'just a little' can kill. There is no safe amount when you can't detect it.
$800
Question:
This DEA finding about counterfeit pills shows how dangerous the current drug supply is.
Answer:
What is that 6 in 10 (60%) of seized fake pills contain a lethal dose of fentanyl?
Discussion Point:
You're essentially playing Russian Roulette with 3–4 chambers loaded. Most people who've taken a fake pill and survived did so by luck — not because they knew what they were doing.
$1,200
Question:
This reason explains why people who have just been released from jail, prison, or a treatment program are at the HIGHEST risk of fentanyl overdose death.
Answer:
What is that their tolerance has dropped during abstinence, so they use their old 'normal' amount and overdose?
Discussion Point:
The first week after release from incarceration has 12 times the overdose risk of the general population. Tolerance resets during abstinence — the dose that used to be 'normal' becomes lethal.
$1,600
Question:
When giving Narcan to someone overdosing on fentanyl (vs. regular heroin), this is different about how many doses you may need.
Answer:
What is you may need 2–3 or more doses, given every 2–3 minutes?
Discussion Point:
Fentanyl's strength means standard naloxone doses are often insufficient. Always give more if the person isn't responding, and CALL 911 — Narcan wears off before fentanyl does.
$2,000
Question:
This fact about how fentanyl is distributed in counterfeit pills explains why one pill from the same batch can be safe while another is lethal.
Answer:
What is that fentanyl isn't evenly mixed — 'hot spots' of concentrated fentanyl exist in the same pill or powder?
Discussion Point:
Two identical-looking pills from the same batch can have wildly different fentanyl content. There's no 'safe' test dose — the safer choice is testing the whole supply.
Understanding Addiction Round 2 · Category 2
$400
Question:
This natural brain chemical — released when you eat good food, achieve a goal, or connect with someone — is the same one that drugs flood the brain with.
Answer:
What is dopamine?
Discussion Point:
Drugs don't create pleasure from nothing — they steal from your brain's own reward system. Over time, natural pleasures stop producing enough dopamine compared to the drug.
$800
Question:
Addiction is described as compulsive drug use DESPITE this — meaning even when the person knows it's hurting them and wants to stop.
Answer:
What is negative consequences (losing jobs, relationships, health, freedom)?
Discussion Point:
The compulsive nature of addiction distinguishes it from habit or choice. Someone in addiction often hates what they're doing and desperately wants to stop, but their brain chemistry makes 'just stopping' extremely difficult.
$1,200
Question:
These three factors — genetic risk, environment (trauma, stress, peer influence), and age of first use — are the major predictors of who develops addiction.
Answer:
What are genetics, environment, and age of first use?
Discussion Point:
None of these alone causes addiction. But together, and especially with early use, the risk dramatically increases. None of this is destiny — understanding risk factors is how you make informed choices.
$1,600
Question:
This brain region — responsible for planning, judgment, and self-control — is still developing until about age 25 and is particularly damaged by drug use during teen years.
Answer:
What is the prefrontal cortex?
Discussion Point:
Your prefrontal cortex is literally under construction until age 25. Drugs damage this region more permanently during development than in adults — meaning poorer judgment lasts longer.
$2,000
Question:
This bidirectional relationship — where trauma increases addiction risk AND addiction increases trauma exposure — creates a cycle that requires both to be treated simultaneously.
Answer:
What is the trauma-addiction cycle?
Discussion Point:
Treating addiction without addressing trauma often fails because the person returns to substances to cope with the trauma symptoms. Integrated trauma-informed addiction treatment has much better outcomes.
Treatment, Recovery & Getting Help Round 2 · Category 3
$400
Question:
This national phone number — available 24/7, free, and confidential — connects you to substance use and mental health support.
Answer:
What is 988 (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline) or 1-800-662-HELP (SAMHSA)?
Discussion Point:
These lines aren't just for emergencies — you can call to ask for advice, information, or help for a friend. No judgment, no cost, real people who care.
$800
Question:
This approach to treatment — meeting people where they are, focusing on reducing harm even if they aren't ready to quit — is called this.
Answer:
What is harm reduction?
Discussion Point:
Harm reduction keeps people alive until they're ready for more. You can't recover if you're dead. It's not giving up on someone — it's keeping the door to recovery open.
$1,200
Question:
This evidence-based therapy — teaching people to identify and change thoughts that lead to drug use, while building skills to cope with triggers — is the most widely used in addiction treatment.
Answer:
What is Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)?
Discussion Point:
CBT teaches you how your thoughts affect your feelings and actions. Noticing a thought pattern BEFORE it leads to using is a skill you can learn — and it works.
$1,600
Question:
This important fact about relapse — which happens to 40–60% of people in recovery — reframes what many see as 'failure.'
Answer:
What is that relapse rates for addiction are similar to rates for diabetes and asthma — it signals a need to adjust the plan, not give up?
Discussion Point:
Relapse is painful, but it is not the end. Most people who achieve lasting recovery have relapsed multiple times. Every attempt at recovery matters and builds toward the next one.
$2,000
Question:
SAMHSA defines recovery as this process — describing not just sobriety but a whole-life transformation.
Answer:
What is 'a process of change through which individuals improve their health and wellness, live a self-directed life, and strive to reach their full potential'?
Discussion Point:
Recovery isn't just 'not using.' It's building a life worth living — health, housing, purpose, connection. The four dimensions of SAMHSA's recovery model: Health, Home, Purpose, Community.
Legal Consequences: Your Future Matters Round 2 · Category 4
$400
Question:
This type of criminal record — created when someone is convicted of a crime — can follow a person for life and affect jobs, housing, college, and custody of children.
Answer:
What is a criminal record or conviction?
Discussion Point:
A conviction doesn't end when you're released. The invisible sentence — discrimination in hiring, housing, and education — can last decades.
$800
Question:
A drug conviction can make you ineligible for this federal financial assistance — making college much harder or impossible to afford.
Answer:
What is federal student aid (FAFSA)?
Discussion Point:
A drug conviction can block you from Pell Grants and federal loans for years. Your education and future are at stake — and most teens don't know this until it's too late.
$1,200
Question:
Selling drugs within this distance from a school, park, or playground typically DOUBLES the criminal penalties.
Answer:
What is 1,000 feet?
Discussion Point:
In many urban areas, you're within 1,000 feet of a school almost everywhere. Enhanced penalty zones affect sentencing dramatically — and most young people don't know this.
$1,600
Question:
This legal protection — available in most states — protects people from drug prosecution when they call 911 for an overdose.
Answer:
What is the Good Samaritan law (or Medical Amnesty law)?
Discussion Point:
Good Samaritan laws vary by state. Most protect the caller AND the person overdosing from arrest for simple possession. Knowing your state's law could literally save a life.
$2,000
Question:
This legal concept — where drug charges can be brought against users who share drugs that lead to a friend's overdose death — is increasingly used to prosecute young people.
Answer:
What is drug-induced homicide laws?
Discussion Point:
Over 25 states have used these laws to charge people with manslaughter or murder when the drugs they gave to a friend caused death. This includes cases between close friends.
Family Impact & Growing Up Around Addiction Round 2 · Category 5
$400
Question:
About this fraction of American children — 1 in this number — lives with at least one parent with a substance use disorder.
Answer:
What is 1 in 8 (about 12% or nearly 9 million children)?
Discussion Point:
You are not alone. Millions of young people are in exactly the situation you may have experienced. Growing up around addiction is not your fault, and it doesn't determine your future.
$800
Question:
Children of parents with alcohol use disorder have this higher risk of developing addiction themselves — due to both genes AND environment.
Answer:
What is 2–9 times higher risk?
Discussion Point:
This isn't fate. Knowing you have higher biological risk is powerful information — it means you have even stronger reasons to be cautious, and means you deserve extra support.
$1,200
Question:
This term describes the false role some children in addicted households take on — acting as the parent, caretaker, or emotional support for a parent.
Answer:
What is parentification (or role reversal)?
Discussion Point:
Taking care of a parent who should be taking care of you is deeply painful and confusing. It can leave lasting effects on your ability to trust, set boundaries, and feel safe. This was not your job.
$1,600
Question:
More than this many children lost a parent to drug overdose between 2011 and 2021 — a traumatic loss with unique grief challenges.
Answer:
What is 321,000 children?
Discussion Point:
Overdose death of a parent is a devastating, stigmatized loss. Grief after addiction-related death is often complicated by anger, shame, and lack of community support. This pain deserves acknowledgment and care.
$2,000
Question:
Children who experienced abuse have this fold higher likelihood of developing their own substance use disorders compared to children without abuse history.
Answer:
What is 4.3 times higher risk?
Discussion Point:
Abuse and addiction are deeply connected because trauma changes how the brain handles stress, pleasure, and risk. This is NOT the child's fault — and it's why trauma treatment is part of addiction treatment.
Decision Making & Real-Life Scenarios Round 2 · Category 6
$400
Question:
Your roommate says 'try this, it's just a pill, it'll chill you out.' The safest response is this.
Answer:
What is saying no — any pill from an unknown source could contain fentanyl?
Discussion Point:
6 in 10 counterfeit pills contain a lethal dose of fentanyl. 'Just a pill' has killed thousands of teens. Your roommate may not know what's actually in it — and their life may be at risk too.
$800
Question:
At a party, you notice your friend is slumped over, not responding, and their lips look bluish. The FIRST thing to do is this.
Answer:
What is call 911 immediately (and give Narcan if available)?
Discussion Point:
Blue lips mean oxygen deprivation. Every minute without oxygen causes brain damage. Don't waste time trying to wake them up first — call 911 while you or someone else gives Narcan.
$1,200
Question:
You've been clean for six months and feeling really confident — almost too confident. Recovery experts call this dangerous state of overconfidence this.
Answer:
What is complacency (or pink cloud syndrome)?
Discussion Point:
Feeling great in early recovery is wonderful — but complacency is one of the top triggers of relapse. Staying connected to support matters MOST when you feel like you don't need it.
$1,600
Question:
Someone asks you to hold something for them or be a lookout while they deal. The legal consequence you need to understand is this.
Answer:
What is that you can be charged with the SAME drug crime as the dealer — including trafficking and distribution?
Discussion Point:
Drug conspiracy laws are broad. 'Holding' drugs for someone makes you a possessor. Being a lookout makes you part of a conspiracy. This isn't worth the loyalty — to anyone.
$2,000
Question:
Research on social influence shows that one of the MOST effective ways to help a peer choose not to use drugs is this — rather than direct advice-giving.
Answer:
What is modeling recovery and healthy behavior yourself (being a living example)?
Discussion Point:
Seeing someone their own age making it without substances is one of the most powerful influences on young people's choices. Your recovery, your choices, and your presence can change someone else's life simply by showing it's possible.

🏆 Final Jeopardy!

Category: Recovery & Hope
This national organization, founded in 1935, has helped millions of people recover from alcoholism using a 12-step program and the principle that people with addiction can help other people with addiction achieve sobriety. It operates in over 180 countries with more than 2 million members and has no dues or fees — only a desire to stop drinking.
What is Alcoholics Anonymous (AA)?
Facilitator Note: Use Final Jeopardy as a moment to talk about hope. Recovery is possible — millions of people have done it. The same peer support that makes AA powerful is available in many forms, including NA, SMART Recovery, peer mentors, and recovery coaches. Ask the group: "What would your life look like in 5 years if things went really well?"
